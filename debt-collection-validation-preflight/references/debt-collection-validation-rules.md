# Debt Collection Validation Rules

Use these rules as a local evidence checklist, not as legal advice, debt settlement advice, credit repair, or a guarantee that a collector will stop collection. Mark current-law or jurisdiction-specific conclusions as "verify before send" when the state, court status, debt type, dates, or current guidance is uncertain.

## Core Decision Axes

- **Collector identity**: Require collector name and mailing address before a written response packet is treated as ready. Phone numbers alone are not enough for a durable paper trail.
- **Validation notice**: Look for current creditor, amount, itemization date or amount basis, consumer rights language, dispute instructions, and a way to request the original creditor.
- **30-day timing**: Track the date validation information was received or assumed received. A written dispute or original-creditor request is strongest when sent within the validation period.
- **Written proof**: Prefer certified mail, tracked mail, portal receipt, or another durable receipt for disputes and original-creditor requests.
- **Debt theory**: Use one narrow, evidence-backed theory per case: unknown debt, not mine, identity theft, paid/settled, wrong amount, old debt, medical collection, original-creditor request, continued collection after dispute, or mixed.
- **Continued collection after dispute**: If a timely written dispute was sent and verification has not been received, flag payment demands, credit-reporting threats, calls, letters, or lawsuit threats for owner/legal review.
- **Old debt caution**: Do not tell the user to pay, promise to pay, or admit debt when the account may be time-barred. Ask for state, last payment/charge date, and legal owner review.
- **Court documents**: A summons, complaint, arbitration notice, or court deadline is a legal-response issue. Preserve facts and route to legal owner review; do not substitute a validation letter for a court answer.
- **Packet hygiene**: Use concise factual language: identify the collector, disputed debt, dates, what information is requested, what is disputed, and which attachments support the request.

## Evidence Checklist

| Scenario | Minimum useful evidence |
|---|---|
| Unknown debt or missing validation notice | Collection letter, envelope or delivery date, call/text/email log, collector name/address, current creditor if shown |
| Not mine or mixed identity | Collection notice, proof of identity/address mismatch, account mismatch facts, credit-report page if reported, identity documents with sensitive fields redacted |
| Identity theft | Collection notice, FTC/police identity-theft report when available, fraud affidavit, account mismatch evidence, mailing receipt |
| Paid or settled | Settlement agreement, payment receipt, canceled check or bank proof with sensitive data redacted, collector/creditor acknowledgment |
| Wrong amount or itemization dispute | Validation notice, itemization table, account statements, insurance/EOB or billing records for medical debt, fee/interest basis |
| Original-creditor request | Validation notice, written request, mailing proof, collector response identifying original creditor |
| Continued collection after written dispute | Written dispute, mailing or portal proof, delivery date, later collector letter/call log/payment demand/credit-reporting threat |
| Old debt or possible time-barred debt | Last payment/charge date, charge-off date, state, collector notice, any lawsuit/court papers, no admission/payment promise in draft |

## Common Failure Modes

- User asks for "proof" but has no collector mailing address or no dated notice.
- Draft misses the 30-day window or fails to send the dispute in writing.
- Case says "not mine" but attaches no account mismatch, identity-theft, or credit-report evidence.
- Case says "paid" but attaches only a bank screenshot with no collector/account tie.
- A collector continued collection after timely dispute, but the packet lacks proof the dispute was mailed or delivered.
- User wants the agent to call, pay, submit a live complaint, or negotiate settlement.
- A court summons is treated like an ordinary collection letter.
- Sensitive files include full SSNs, full IDs, bank credentials, full account numbers, private keys, tokens, or unrelated third-party records.
